## 1. PURPOSE AND OVERVIEW

This document provides practical guidance for conducting effective, legal, and consistent interviews at Weaver Enterprises Inc. These guidelines help hiring managers and interview team members:

- Conduct structured, competency-based interviews
- Evaluate candidates fairly and objectively
- Comply with employment laws
- Create positive candidate experiences
- Make informed hiring decisions

All employees participating in interviews must review and follow these guidelines.

## 2. INTERVIEW PREPARATION

### 2.1 Before the Interview

**Review Materials:**
- Read candidate's resume and application thoroughly
- Review job description and key requirements
- Understand the role's essential functions
- Review any assessments or work samples submitted

**Prepare Questions:**
- Develop 5-8 core questions focused on key competencies
- Use behavioral questions ("Tell me about a time when...")
- Prepare follow-up probes
- Coordinate with other interviewers to avoid duplication

**Logistics:**
- Confirm interview time and location
- Reserve conference room or set up video call
- Allocate sufficient time (typically 45-60 minutes)
- Plan to arrive 5 minutes early
- Have water available for in-person interviews

**Know What You're Evaluating:**
- Identify 3-4 key competencies for the role
- Understand must-have vs. nice-to-have qualifications
- Clarify decision criteria with hiring manager
- Review compensation range

### 2.2 Creating a Welcoming Environment

**In-Person Interviews:**
- Greet candidate warmly
- Offer water or coffee
- Provide overview of interview format
- Put candidate at ease with brief small talk

**Virtual Interviews:**
- Test technology in advance
- Send calendar invite with video link
- Join video call 2-3 minutes early
- Ensure professional background and lighting
- Minimize distractions

## 3. INTERVIEW STRUCTURE

### 3.1 Recommended Interview Flow

**Opening (5 minutes):**
- Welcome and introductions
- Brief overview of interview format and timing
- Ask if candidate has any questions before starting

**Experience and Background (10-15 minutes):**
- Walk through resume and career progression
- Clarify dates, roles, and responsibilities
- Understand career motivations and transitions

**Competency-Based Questions (25-30 minutes):**
- Ask prepared behavioral questions
- Use STAR method to evaluate responses
- Probe for depth and specifics
- Take notes on responses

**Role and Company Discussion (5-10 minutes):**
- Describe the position and expectations
- Explain team structure and reporting relationships
- Share information about company culture and values
- Discuss growth opportunities

**Candidate Questions (5-10 minutes):**
- Give candidate time to ask questions
- Answer honestly and thoroughly
- Note quality and depth of questions asked

**Closing (2-3 minutes):**
- Explain next steps and timeline
- Provide contact information
- Thank candidate for their time

### 3.2 Interview Timing

**Typical Interview Lengths:**
- Phone screen: 20-30 minutes
- First-round interview: 45-60 minutes
- Second-round interview: 60-90 minutes (may include multiple interviewers)
- Final interview: 30-60 minutes
- Panel interview: 60-90 minutes

Start and end on time. If running over, ask candidate if they can stay longer.

## 4. BEHAVIORAL INTERVIEWING TECHNIQUES

### 4.1 What is Behavioral Interviewing

Behavioral interviewing is based on the principle that past behavior is the best predictor of future performance. Instead of hypothetical questions, ask candidates to describe specific situations from their experience.

**Traditional Question:**
"How would you handle a difficult customer?"

**Behavioral Question:**
"Tell me about a time when you dealt with a difficult customer. What was the situation and how did you handle it?"

### 4.2 The STAR Method

Evaluate responses using the STAR framework:

**S - Situation:**
- What was the context?
- When and where did this occur?
- Who was involved?

**T - Task:**
- What was the challenge or goal?
- What was the candidate's role?
- What needed to be accomplished?

**A - Action:**
- What specific actions did the candidate take?
- What was their thought process?
- How did they approach the problem?

**R - Result:**
- What was the outcome?
- What did they learn?
- What would they do differently?
- Were there measurable results?

### 4.3 Sample Behavioral Questions by Competency

**Problem Solving:**
- "Describe a complex problem you faced at work. How did you approach solving it?"
- "Tell me about a time when you had to make a decision without having all the information you needed."

**Teamwork and Collaboration:**
- "Give me an example of a successful team project you contributed to. What was your role?"
- "Describe a time when you had to work with a difficult team member. How did you handle it?"

**Leadership:**
- "Tell me about a time when you had to motivate a team during a challenging period."
- "Describe a situation where you had to give difficult feedback to someone."

**Adaptability:**
- "Describe a time when you had to quickly adapt to a significant change at work."
- "Tell me about a project where the requirements or direction changed midstream."

**Communication:**
- "Give me an example of a time when you had to explain something complex to someone unfamiliar with the topic."
- "Describe a situation where you had to influence someone without direct authority."

**Initiative:**
- "Tell me about a time when you identified a problem and took action without being asked."
- "Describe a project or idea you initiated on your own."

**Time Management:**
- "Describe a situation where you had multiple deadlines competing for your attention. How did you prioritize?"
- "Tell me about a time when you had to complete a project with a tight deadline."

**Conflict Resolution:**
- "Describe a disagreement you had with a coworker. How did you resolve it?"
- "Tell me about a time when you had to mediate between two people who disagreed."

### 4.4 Probing Questions

If a candidate's initial response lacks detail, use probing questions:

- "Can you tell me more about that?"
- "What specifically did you do?"
- "What was your exact role in that situation?"
- "What were you thinking at that moment?"
- "How did others react?"
- "What was the outcome?"
- "What would you do differently next time?"

Don't accept vague or generalized answers. Dig deeper to understand the candidate's actual contribution and decision-making.

### 4.5 Red Flags in Responses

**Watch for:**
- Vague answers without specifics
- Speaking in "we" instead of "I" (unable to articulate personal contribution)
- Cannot provide concrete examples
- Blames others without taking accountability
- Inconsistencies in story
- Exaggerates or embellishes
- Negative attitude about former employers or colleagues

## 5. LEGAL COMPLIANCE

### 5.1 Prohibited Questions

**Never Ask About:**
- Age or date of birth
- Race, ethnicity, or national origin
- Religion or religious observances
- Marital status, pregnancy, or family plans
- Sexual orientation or gender identity
- Disabilities or medical conditions
- Genetic information
- Arrest records (conviction records are permissible if job-related)
- Military discharge status
- Citizenship (may ask about work authorization)
- Financial status (unless directly job-related for financial positions)

### 5.2 Acceptable vs. Unacceptable Questions

| Topic | UNACCEPTABLE | ACCEPTABLE |
|-------|-------------|------------|
| Age | "How old are you?" | "Are you over 18?" (if required for job) |
| Family | "Do you have children?" "Are you planning to have children?" | "Are you able to work required hours/travel?" (ask all candidates) |
| Disability | "Do you have any disabilities?" | "Can you perform the essential functions of this job with or without accommodation?" |
| National Origin | "Where were you born?" "What's your native language?" | "Are you authorized to work in the United States?" |
| Religion | "What religion do you practice?" "What holidays do you observe?" | "Are you available to work [specific schedule/days]?" (ask all candidates) |
| Arrest Record | "Have you ever been arrested?" | "Have you been convicted of [specific crime relevant to job]?" |
| Military | "What type of discharge did you receive?" | "What type of training/experience did you gain in the military?" |

### 5.3 Discussing Accommodations

You **may** ask: "Can you perform the essential functions of this job with or without reasonable accommodation?"

If candidate discloses a need for accommodation:
- Discuss what accommodations might be needed
- Engage in interactive process
- Do not ask about nature or severity of disability
- Do not ask about medical history
- Refer to HR if questions arise

### 5.4 Work Authorization

You **may** ask: "Are you authorized to work in the United States?"

You **may not** ask:
- "Are you a U.S. citizen?"
- "Where were you born?"
- "What is your immigration status?"

All candidates must complete Form I-9 after hire (not during interview).

## 6. EVALUATION AND DECISION MAKING

### 6.1 Note-Taking

**Do:**
- Take notes during interview
- Record specific examples and responses
- Note both strengths and concerns
- Document relevant job-related observations

**Don't:**
- Write notes about protected characteristics
- Record subjective judgments unrelated to job ("too old," "won't fit in")
- Make comments about appearance, accent, or personal characteristics
- Write anything you wouldn't want a lawyer to read in court

**Remember:** Your notes may be requested in legal proceedings. Keep them professional and job-focused.

### 6.2 Evaluation Criteria

**Rate candidates on:**
- Technical skills and qualifications
- Relevant experience
- Cultural fit and values alignment
- Communication skills
- Problem-solving ability
- Specific competencies required for role

**Use standardized rating scale:**
- 1 - Does not meet requirements
- 2 - Partially meets requirements
- 3 - Meets requirements
- 4 - Exceeds requirements
- 5 - Far exceeds requirements

### 6.3 Completing Interview Feedback

**Submit feedback within 24 hours:**
- Complete evaluation form in applicant tracking system
- Provide ratings for each competency area
- Include specific examples supporting ratings
- Note any concerns or red flags
- Recommend: advance, reject, or need more information

**Feedback should be:**
- Specific and evidence-based
- Job-related
- Objective
- Balanced (strengths and weaknesses)
- Free from bias or stereotypes

### 6.4 Avoiding Bias

**Common Biases to Avoid:**

**Halo/Horn Effect:**
- Don't let one positive trait overshadow weaknesses (halo)
- Don't let one negative trait overshadow strengths (horn)

**Similar-to-Me Bias:**
- Don't favor candidates who share your background, interests, or experiences
- Focus on job requirements, not personal similarities

**First Impression Bias:**
- Don't make snap judgments in first 5 minutes
- Give candidates fair opportunity to demonstrate qualifications

**Contrast Effect:**
- Don't compare candidates to each other
- Evaluate each against job requirements independently

**Confirmation Bias:**
- Don't look only for information confirming initial opinion
- Remain open to evidence contradicting first impression

**Recency Bias:**
- Don't give more weight to most recent interviews
- Review notes from all candidates when making decision

**Strategies to Reduce Bias:**
- Use structured interviews with same questions for all candidates
- Take detailed notes rather than relying on memory
- Use diverse interview panels
- Focus on job-related competencies
- Make decisions based on evidence, not gut feeling

## 7. SPECIAL INTERVIEW SITUATIONS

### 7.1 Panel Interviews

**Benefits:**
- Multiple perspectives on candidate
- More efficient than sequential interviews
- Reduces individual bias

**Best Practices:**
- Designate lead interviewer to facilitate
- Assign questions to specific panelists in advance
- Avoid talking over each other
- Give candidate time to respond fully
- Debrief immediately after interview

### 7.2 Virtual Interviews

**Preparation:**
- Test technology (camera, microphone, internet)
- Use professional background or blur feature
- Ensure good lighting
- Minimize background noise and distractions
- Have backup plan (phone number) if tech fails

**During Interview:**
- Make eye contact by looking at camera
- Speak clearly and at moderate pace
- Be mindful of lag time
- Watch for visual cues that candidate wants to speak
- Use screen sharing if reviewing documents or portfolio

**Considerations:**
- Some candidates less comfortable with video
- May be technical difficulties beyond candidate's control
- Harder to build rapport virtually
- Give grace for minor technical issues

### 7.3 Phone Screenings

**Purpose:**
- Initial screening before investing time in full interview
- Assess basic qualifications and interest
- Clarify resume questions
- Gauge communication skills

**Structure:**
- 15-30 minutes maximum
- Focus on key qualifications
- Explain role and expectations
- Discuss salary range and availability
- Determine if candidate should advance

**Tips:**
- Eliminate distractions
- Have resume and job description in front of you
- Take notes
- Listen actively
- Be prepared to sell opportunity if strong candidate

### 7.4 Interviewing Internal Candidates

**Special Considerations:**
- May have more knowledge of company/role
- Evaluate objectively against external candidates
- Consider current performance and potential
- Discuss transition timeline if selected
- Handle rejection sensitively to preserve relationship

**Don't:**
- Assume you know internal candidate's capabilities
- Give internal candidate unfair advantage
- Skip standard interview process
- Make promises about outcome

### 7.5 Interviewing Former Employees

**What to Consider:**
- Reason for previous departure
- Performance in previous role
- Time since departure
- Changes in person or company
- Manager's perspective on return

**Questions to Ask:**
- "Why are you interested in returning to Weaver Enterprises?"
- "What did you learn from your time away?"
- "What would you do differently this time?"
- "What attracted you to this specific role?"

### 7.6 Campus and Entry-Level Recruiting

**Adjust Approach:**
- Focus on potential over experience
- Ask about coursework, projects, internships
- Evaluate learning agility and enthusiasm
- Discuss career development opportunities
- Provide more information about company and role

**Questions for New Graduates:**
- "Describe a challenging academic project. How did you approach it?"
- "Tell me about a time you balanced multiple priorities (school, work, activities)."
- "What have you learned from your internship experiences?"
- "Why are you interested in this field/industry?"

## 8. SELLING THE OPPORTUNITY

### 8.1 Why Selling Matters

Strong candidates often have multiple offers. Part of your role is to:
- Generate excitement about the opportunity
- Showcase company culture and values
- Explain growth and development possibilities
- Build relationship with candidate

### 8.2 What to Highlight

**About the Role:**
- Impactful work and key responsibilities
- Opportunities for growth and advancement
- Learning and development support
- Team dynamics and collaboration

**About the Company:**
- Mission and values
- Market position and growth trajectory
- Innovation and industry leadership
- Culture and employee engagement
- Work-life balance and flexibility
- Awards and recognition

**About the Team:**
- Team structure and dynamics
- Collaborative environment
- Diversity of backgrounds and perspectives
- Support and mentorship

### 8.3 Answering Candidate Questions Honestly

**Be Truthful About:**
- Challenges of the role
- Areas where company is still developing
- Work pace and demands
- Why position is open

**Don't:**
- Overpromise on advancement or compensation
- Misrepresent culture or expectations
- Dismiss legitimate concerns
- Provide inaccurate information

Setting accurate expectations leads to better long-term fit and retention.

## 9. AFTER THE INTERVIEW

### 9.1 Debrief Meeting

**When to Debrief:**
- Immediately after interview while impressions are fresh
- Within 24 hours maximum

**What to Discuss:**
- Overall impressions
- Specific strengths and concerns
- Evidence for and against hiring
- Comparison to job requirements
- Cultural fit assessment
- Recommendation (advance, reject, need more info)

**Decision-Making:**
- Hiring manager makes final decision
- Considers all feedback from interview team
- HR provides guidance on process and compliance
- Hiring manager notifies HR of decision

### 9.2 Documenting Decisions

**In Applicant Tracking System:**
- Record all feedback and ratings
- Document reasons for advancing or rejecting
- Note any concerns that need further exploration
- Keep decisions job-related and objective

**Audit Trail:**
- System maintains history of decisions
- Ensures accountability and consistency
- Supports defense if hiring decision questioned

### 9.3 Reference Checks

If moving forward with candidate:
- Conduct reference checks before offer
- Ask structured questions about performance
- Listen for what's not said
- Verify employment dates and titles
- Ask about rehire eligibility

## 10. COMMON INTERVIEW MISTAKES

### 10.1 Mistakes to Avoid

**Talking Too Much:**
- Interview should be 70% candidate talking, 30% interviewer
- Don't monopolize conversation
- Give candidate time to fully answer

**Leading Questions:**
- Don't suggest desired answer
- Bad: "We value teamwork here. Are you a team player?"
- Good: "Describe your experience working in teams."

**Not Listening:**
- Don't mentally prepare next question while candidate speaks
- Focus on what candidate is actually saying
- Take notes to help remember details

**Making Snap Judgments:**
- Don't decide in first 5 minutes
- Give candidate full interview to demonstrate qualifications
- Evaluate based on entire interview, not first impression

**Forgetting to Sell:**
- Don't treat interview as interrogation
- Balance evaluation with selling opportunity
- Show enthusiasm for role and company

**Not Preparing:**
- Don't wing it
- Review materials and prepare questions
- Understand role requirements

**Asking Hypothetical Questions:**
- Don't ask "What would you do if..."
- Ask for actual examples: "Tell me about a time when..."

**Allowing Interruptions:**
- Don't check phone or email during interview
- Give candidate full attention
- Close door and minimize distractions

## 11. CANDIDATE REJECTION GUIDELINES

### 11.1 When to Reject Candidates

**Post-Application Review:**
- Does not meet minimum qualifications
- Resume shows significant unexplained gaps
- Application contains false information
- Compensation expectations far exceed budget

**Post-Phone Screen:**
- Basic qualifications not as strong as indicated on resume
- Poor communication skills for role requirements
- Lack of genuine interest in position
- Availability doesn't match needs

**Post-Interview:**
- Insufficient experience or skills
- Poor cultural fit
- Negative references
- Better qualified candidates in pipeline
- Concerns about reliability or professionalism

**Post-Background Check:**
- Failed background check (see Recruitment & Hiring Policy)
- Failed drug test (for applicable positions)
- Unable to verify credentials
- Discrepancies in employment history

### 11.2 Timing of Rejection Communication

**Prompt Notification:**
- Post-resume review: Within 2 weeks of application
- Post-phone screen: Within 1 week
- Post-first interview: Within 1-2 weeks
- Post-final interview: Within 1 week

**Why Timely Communication Matters:**
- Shows respect for candidate's time
- Maintains positive employer brand
- Allows candidates to pursue other opportunities
- Reflects professional organization

### 11.3 Methods of Communication

**Email Rejection (Appropriate for):**
- Early-stage candidates (application review, phone screen)
- High-volume recruiting situations
- When candidate relationship is minimal

**Phone Call Rejection (Recommended for):**
- Final-round candidates
- Internal candidates
- When lengthy interview process
- When candidate asked for feedback
- When candidate was a strong contender

**In-Person Rejection (Reserved for):**
- Internal candidates (when appropriate)
- When candidate is well-known to organization
- Executive-level positions

### 11.4 What to Say in Rejection Communications

**Key Messages to Include:**
- Thank candidate for their time and interest
- Indicate decision has been made
- Communicate decision clearly (don't leave false hope)
- Encourage future applications if appropriate
- Maintain professional, respectful tone

### 11.5 Providing Feedback

**General Guidelines:**
- Not legally required to provide feedback
- Use caution when providing detailed feedback
- Keep feedback high-level and constructive
- Avoid statements that could be legally problematic

**When to Provide Feedback:**
- Final-round candidates who request it
- Internal candidates (with appropriate care)
- When there's a developmental relationship

**When to Decline Feedback:**
- Legal concerns about discrimination claims
- Concerns candidate will argue or become hostile
- No clear, objective basis for decision
- Company policy prohibits (consult HR)

**How to Provide Constructive Feedback:**

**Do:**
- Focus on qualifications and fit, not personality
- Provide specific, job-related observations
- Frame as developmental areas
- Suggest areas for growth
- Keep it brief (2-3 key points maximum)

**Don't:**
- Provide exhaustive list of deficiencies
- Make it personal or attack character
- Compare to other candidates
- Discuss protected characteristics
- Put anything in writing that could be misinterpreted

**Sample Feedback Statements:**

✅ GOOD:
- "We were looking for someone with more direct experience managing enterprise-level implementations."
- "The role requires extensive public speaking, and we selected a candidate with a stronger presentation background."
- "We needed someone who could start immediately, and another candidate had better availability."

❌ AVOID:
- "You're not a good cultural fit." (Too vague, could imply bias)
- "You seemed nervous and not confident enough." (Subjective, could relate to disability)
- "We went with someone younger and more energetic." (Age discrimination)
- "We needed someone more technical." (If said to a woman in tech, could imply gender bias)

### 11.6 Handling Difficult Rejection Conversations

**If Candidate Becomes Emotional:**
- Remain calm and empathetic
- Allow them to express feelings
- Don't argue or become defensive
- Reiterate appreciation for their interest
- End conversation professionally if becomes inappropriate

**If Candidate Argues or Challenges Decision:**
- Remain professional and firm
- Restate that decision is final
- Don't provide additional detail or justification
- Don't be drawn into debate
- End call if necessary: "I understand you're disappointed, but the decision is final. I wish you the best in your search."

**If Candidate Threatens Legal Action:**
- Remain calm and professional
- Don't admit fault or apologize excessively
- Document conversation immediately
- Report to HR and Legal immediately
- Do not continue to engage with candidate

**If Candidate Asks About Discrimination:**
- Take concern seriously
- Document specific allegations
- Refer to HR immediately
- Don't dismiss or argue
- Don't provide detailed defense

### 11.7 Rejection of Internal Candidates

**Special Considerations:**
- More sensitive due to ongoing employment relationship
- Risk of disengagement or resignation
- May impact team morale
- Requires thoughtful communication

**Best Practices:**

**Timing:**
- Notify before external candidates
- Don't let them hear through grapevine
- Allow time to process before announcement

**Communication:**
- Phone call or in-person (not email)
- Private setting
- Sufficient time for discussion
- Involve current manager if appropriate

**Message:**
- Be direct but compassionate
- Acknowledge their strengths and contributions
- Explain decision objectively
- Discuss development opportunities
- Reaffirm value to organization in current role
- Discuss path forward

**Follow-Up:**
- Check in within a week
- Offer development planning
- Discuss future opportunities
- Monitor engagement and morale

**Sample Internal Rejection Script:**

"[Name], I wanted to talk with you about the [Position Title] role you interviewed for. First, I want to thank you for putting yourself forward and for the time you invested in the process.

After careful consideration, we've decided to move forward with another candidate. This was a difficult decision because you have [specific strengths]. Ultimately, we selected someone with [specific differentiator relevant to role].

I want to be clear that this doesn't diminish the value you bring in your current role. Your work on [specific contributions] has been excellent, and you're an important part of the team.

I'd like to discuss how we can support your development and career growth here at Weaver Enterprises. Are you open to having that conversation?"

### 11.8 Maintaining Candidate Relationships

**Why It Matters:**
- Future job openings may be better fit
- Candidate may refer others
- Candidate may become customer or partner
- Protects employer brand and reputation

**Silver Medal Program:**
- Keep strong candidates warm for future openings
- Add to talent pipeline or talent community
- Notify of relevant future opportunities
- Maintain relationship through periodic touchpoints

**Candidate Reapplication:**
- Welcome qualified candidates to reapply
- Review with fresh perspective
- Don't hold past rejection against them
- Consider growth since last application

### 11.9 Legal Considerations

**Document Everything:**
- Reason for rejection must be job-related
- Document in applicant tracking system
- Keep notes professional and objective
- Consistent treatment across candidates

**Avoid Discriminatory Reasons:**
- Never reject based on protected characteristics
- Ensure reason is legitimate and non-discriminatory
- Apply same standards to all candidates
- Be prepared to defend decision if challenged

**Retain Records:**
- Keep all application materials per retention policy
- Maintain interview notes and evaluations
- Document communication with candidate
- Store securely and confidentially

**Red Flags Requiring HR/Legal Review:**
- Candidate threatens legal action
- Decision could appear discriminatory
- Internal candidate with performance issues
- High-profile or executive-level rejection
- Unusual circumstances or complications
